# Imo i Nicro — growth approach

How this grows from a polished A1 foundation into the thing it's for: **two
people actually holding a conversation in Croatian** — standard Croatian as the
backbone, with *their* Dalmatian known item by item. A living document: the
direction, the principles, and what to build next. Correct it freely; it's a
map, not a contract.

---

## 1. What this is (and what it isn't)

It is a **shared language project for two specific people**, tied to Dalmatian
heritage, used a little every day, aimed at conversation — and, over time, an
artifact of that shared effort: their words, their family's phrasings, their
journey.

It is **not** a generic A2 course and not a small Duolingo. Its strengths are
that it is *small, personal, sourced, Croatian-not-Serbian, Dalmatian-aware, and
theirs.* Every addition is measured against one question: **does this help the
two of them talk to each other (and to family) in Croatian?** If not, it doesn't
go in — no matter how "complete" it would make the course look.

---

## 2. Principles (the guardrails)

- **Function-driven, not a grammar checklist.** Each increment is a real thing
  they'd do together — catch up, make a plan, order, talk about the day, call
  family — and pulls in only the words and grammar that function needs. Plurals
  arrive when "my friends" needs them; the imperative when "pass me / let's go"
  needs it.
- **Standard is the backbone; Dalmatian is the delta.** Teach štokavian standard
  as the spine (it's what's written, understood everywhere, and what the sources
  cover), and surface where Dalmatian differs as a tagged parallel — never mixed
  into the "correct" standard answer. (See §5.)
- **Teach-first stays.** Nothing is tested before it's taught (the app's spine).
- **Sourced and authentic.** Keep the per-item citations and the
  Croatian-vs-Serbian distinctions. That discipline is what makes it trustworthy.
- **Half authored spine, half theirs.** Seed a strong conversational core and the
  well-known Dalmatian differences; let the two of them grow and correct the rest
  through the flag/override and variety tools that already exist. It must never
  become a second job to maintain.
- **The relationship is the engine.** Lean into co-op (shared goals) over pure
  competition; design content to be *run between the two of them.*
- **Lean engine.** Resist infrastructure creep. The whole plan needs ~one new
  content type and ~one new exercise type. No distributed-systems work.
- **Stays theirs.** Portable, exportable, lasting. It's a record of a
  relationship, not a disposable app.

---

## 3. The keystone: dialogue + comprehension

Everything conversational rides on one missing capability. Today the app trains
you to *produce* isolated sentences; it never trains you to *understand what
comes back and reply.* A conversation is turn-taking.

So the first structural piece — the only real engine work in the plan — is:

- a **dialogue** content type: a 2–4 turn exchange; and
- one new exercise type: **prompt → reply** — hear/read a line, choose or produce
  the right response (`"Kako si?"` → `"Dobro, hvala. A ti?"`), distractors drawn
  from other replies.

It reuses the existing exercise rendering and FSRS scheduling. Build it first;
every later increment is just content poured into it.

---

## 4. The content ladder (A1 → conversation)

Sequenced by how much real conversation each unlocks. Each increment also seeds
the Dalmatian differences (§5) for the words it introduces.

1. **Asking & answering** — consolidate the question words
   (tko/što/gdje/kada/kako/zašto/koliko), add `ali`/`ili`/`jer` (five words that
   multiply expressiveness), ship the dialogue engine, ~15 short exchanges.
   *Moves them from stating to exchanging — the biggest single jump.*
2. **Plans & requests** — the imperative daily life runs on (`daj mi`, `dođi`,
   `idemo`, `čekaj`), future tense exercised inside dialogue. "What shall we do."
3. **Plurals & quantity** — nominative plural, "my friends / we / two brothers",
   shopping in plural. The missing system, introduced where function demands it.
4. **The day & feelings** — more everyday verbs/adjectives, past-tense dialogue
   ("how was your day"), so the perfekt they already know gets *used* in exchange.
5. **Family & heritage (personal)** — their actual people and their region's
   words; the "our phrases" deck as a first-class feature. Where it stops being a
   course and becomes *theirs.* This is the most Dalmatian-heavy unit.
6. **Opinions & longer turns** — `jer`/`zato` because-clauses, "mislim da…",
   stringing turns together → genuine B1-ish conversation.

---

## 5. Dalmatian alongside standard

The two of them speak Dalmatian. The aim is standard Croatian as the backbone
*and* knowing, word by word, where Dalmatian diverges — so Dalmatian is a
first-class parallel track, built on the variety layer that already exists (the
green tagged chips), elevated and pre-seeded rather than left empty.

Three axes of difference, each taught as a delta on top of the standard item:

1. **Pronunciation — ikavian.** Standard is (i)jekavian; Dalmatian is largely
   *ikavian*: old "yat" becomes **i**, not *ije/je*. dijete→**dite**,
   mlijeko→**mliko**, lijep→**lip**, vrijeme→**vrime**, mjesto→**misto**,
   čovjek→**čovik**, gdje→**di**, nedjelja→**nedilja**, djeca→**dica**. One rule
   recolours a large share of the vocabulary — worth teaching as a *pattern*,
   with each affected word carrying its Dalmatian form.
2. **Lexicon — the coastal/Venetian words.** Centuries of Venetian rule left a
   Romance vocabulary the mainland doesn't use: šugaman (ručnik/towel), pjat
   (tanjur/plate), pirun (vilica/fork), tavaja (stolnjak/tablecloth), ponistra
   (prozor/window), kantun (kut/corner), špina (slavina/tap), spiza (groceries),
   bevanda (wine cut with water), ćakula (a chat), fjaka (coastal languor),
   bonaca (a dead-calm sea), and the winds bura / jugo / maestral. These are the
   words that make speech *sound* Dalmatian.
3. **Common habits.** Word-final -m often becomes -n (sam→**san**, vidim→**vidin**);
   infinitives drop the final -i (raditi→**radit**, spavati→**spavat**); plus
   assorted contractions. (Full island Čakavian — *ča* for *što*, and more —
   varies place to place; treat it as island/older speech, not pan-Dalmatian.)

In the app: each standard word/sentence can carry its Dalmatian counterpart,
shown as a separately tagged variant (never mixed into the standard answer), with
a note on which axis it differs on. Seed the well-known differences; then the
flag/variety workflow lets the two of them correct them to *their family's*
actual usage — the real target, which only they know. (`fjaka` is already in
their data — exactly the kind of word this track is for.)

## 6. Broader dimensions (beyond content)

Content quality is necessary but not sufficient. What actually gets two people to
conversation is sustained daily use and the language mattering to them. These
grow *alongside* the ladder, woven in where an increment calls for them — not all
at once.

- **Motivation without a deadline.** There's no trip or date driving this, so the
  engine is the shared daily habit and each other — the streak and duel already
  do this. Reinforce it with **capability milestones** as intrinsic markers ("you
  can now order a full meal", "you can get through a call with baka in
  Dalmatian"), and lean **co-op** (shared goals the two of them clear together)
  over pure competition.
- **Real voice, not just TTS.** Let them attach a real recording — their own or a
  family member's — to a card. "Hear baka say it" is the most personal and most
  motivating feature available, and for Dalmatian it's also the most *accurate*
  source: the synthesised voice speaks standard, not the coast.
- **Speaking, the untouched skill.** Production is typed today. Add a say-it-aloud
  step, and Croatian speech recognition where the browser supports it. Speaking is
  where solo learners plateau.
- **The two-learner asymmetry as a feature.** If one already speaks more, let the
  stronger partner record audio, set challenges, and field "explain this to your
  partner" tasks — teaching is how the teacher consolidates.
- **A bridge to real input.** A path from controlled sentences → graded authentic
  Croatian (a short text, lyrics, a children's book, real messages), so they can
  eventually understand language the app didn't write.
- **Gentle adaptivity.** FSRS schedules *when* to review; this notices *what* each
  person struggles with (a sound, a case, listening vs. reading) and leans there.
  Lightweight, not a model.
- **Culture as the door.** Heritage language is a way into customs, food, places,
  family. The word notes already do a little of this ("ići na kavu", the bakery
  ritual); let a light cultural layer grow — Dalmatian especially — so the
  language stays meaningful, not just functional.

---

## 7. Sequencing & what *not* to do

- **Phase 1:** the dialogue/comprehension engine + increment #1 (Asking &
  answering). The keystone; everything reuses it. In parallel, seed the Dalmatian
  delta (§5) onto the existing ~160 A1 words — immediately useful and concrete.
- **Phases 2+:** the ladder, one increment at a time, each a shippable chunk the
  two of them can start using immediately, each seeding its Dalmatian differences.
- **Broader features enter where they serve the current increment** — real audio
  when building the family unit; speech practice once there's enough to say.
- **Split `content.js` by level** (`content-a1.js`, `content-a2.js`) only when it
  gets unwieldy — a small refactor, not a prerequisite.
- **Do not** chase A2 completeness for its own sake, add gamification beyond the
  streak/duel, or grow the sync/infra further. The middle of the build already
  over-invested there; the leverage now is content and the two people using it.

---

## 8. Open questions

Decided:
- **No goal/date** — motivation rests on the shared daily habit, not a countdown.
- **Dialect: Dalmatian** — learn standard as the backbone and the Dalmatian delta
  (ikavian pronunciation, coastal lexicon, common habits), refined to the family's
  actual usage through the flag/variety tools.

Still open (they shape the later ladder, not the keystone):
- **How asymmetric are the two learners** — how much does each already know? Sets
  how hard to lean on the "stronger partner teaches" design and the test-out.
- **How much do they want to author themselves** vs. receive a finished course?
- **Which specific Dalmatian features does the family actually use** — filled in
  over time through use and the variety layer, not decided up front.
